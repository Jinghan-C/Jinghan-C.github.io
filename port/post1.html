Preserving justice and love in imagination

Camus vs. Heaney

Preserving justice and love in imagination

By Jinghan Cao June 2020

Camus proposes an idea that “the long fight for justice exhausts the love that nevertheless gave birth to it”. Meanwhile, Camus and Heaney argue that, given the absurdity of the world, one can only persevere without surrendering either love or justice by using imagination. Imagination is where people can return to the sources and re-evaluate the meaning of love and justice. This idea can be evident in Camus’ description of the transcendent moment in Return to Tipasa, Dr. Rieux’s imaginative escape from reality in the Plague and Heaney’s imaginative memory in his poem Clearances. By providing this potential solution, both Camus and Heaney are implying the absurdity of the real world, as justice and love are always conflicting; and suggest that using imagination to revitalize love is actually one’s realization of the absurdity.

Camus argues that persevering without surrendering either love or justice can only be done through imagination. This idea is evident in Camus’ pictures of the transcendent moment in his essay Return to Tipasa. Tipasa is an old Roman city now in ruins. Camus spent quite a lot of time in Tipasa when he was young, and this essay is mainly about his second visit to Tipasa after World War II. In Tipasa, Camus returns to nature and sees the sea first “rose slowly in the ill-defined bay, but the surface of the water seemed almost motionless under the steady rain”(Return to Tipasa, pg195) Here, Camus imagines himself being the sea. Like the sea which is “motionless”, Camus stopped feeling. He came back to Tipasa to reconnect with the world as it is a place with his happy memories. After the transcendent experience, he now “listened to the happy torrents rising within me”(Return to Tipasa, pg 201) Being the sea, which has “happy torrents”, Camus now has revitalized his feelings. In this imagination, he experiences a transcendent moment. He “listened to an almost forgotten sound within myself as if my heart, long stopped, were calmly beginning to beat again. And awake now, I recognized one by one the imperceptible sounds of which the silence was made up.” (Return to Tipasa, pg.201) During this second visit, Camus describes this transcendent moment where his stopped heart beats again and he hears the sound of silence. This transcendental experience is in his imagination, his heart did not stop, nor can he hear the silent sound. These are referring to the source of his feelings and emotions as he has lost them while fighting for justice. Here, by saying his heart “beat again”, Camus is saying that love is reanimated within him and he can finally perceive and have feelings for himself and the world. In this transcendent moment, he regains what he should have and becomes more the person he should be. By returning to Tipase and imagining himself being a sea in Tipasa, Camus is going back to the source of love both physically and mentally. After experiencing the war and French Resistance, Camus’ feelings are exhausted. Through the imagination of being a sea, he regenerates love. During the mental fight for justice, Camus realizes the absurdity of the world as he could not retain the love and other feelings while still obtaining justice. Given the conflict position between love and justice, He reanimates love through the transcendent moment in his imagination.

The idea of using imagination to reanimate love while still maintaining justice is also evident in Camus’ The Plague, specifically in the description of Rieux’s imaginative escape from the disease. Rieux and Tarrou meet up one day to visit a patient and end up having a talk at the terrace of a patient's house. Being a doctor, Rieux’s feelings are exhausted by the plague: “No resource was left him but to tighten the stranglehold on his feelings and harden his heart protectively. For he knew this was the only way of carrying on” (Camus, The Plague, chapter19) Aftering seeing so many people die, the only way for Rieux to continue his work is to hide his feelings. “Tighten his feelings” is the only way to protect himself and to better help others. Realizing that he could not continue without surrendering either justice or love, Rieux knows he has to choose only one of them and he chooses justice. He chooses to continue to help people because it is his job and it is the right thing to do. Luckily, he reanimates love through imagination. While they are swimming after the friendship talk, Rieux imagines them being “isolated from the world, at last free of the town and of the plague”(The Plague, chapter24). The imagination brings Rieux back to the time before the plague where he still has passion and hope for life. The imaginative escape from the plague brings him to the source of love. He finally gets a chance to take a break in this fight and think for himself. In the imagination, without the exhausting fight with the plague, “Rieux could feel under his hand the gnarled, weather-worn visage of the rocks, and a strange happiness possessed him...a happiness that forgot nothing, not even murder”(The Plague, chapter24) Here, “strange” shows that Rieux has lost happiness for so long that he is unfamiliar with it now. “A happiness that forgot nothing” shows one can maintain love without giving up justice. In the imaginative escape from reality, Rieux discovers a way to obtain both love and justice. Rieux chooses to be a doctor because he has love and he wants to save people. But during the fight with the plague, he becomes indifferent and loses the love for not only the people, but also the world. The plague makes him realize the absurdity of the world. Realizing that the absurd reality prevents people from obtaining both love and justice, Rieux regains love through imaginative escape from the plague.

Seamus Heaney also argues the same idea in his poem Clearances where through imagination, one can persevere without surrendering either love or justice. Heaney writes the poem in memory of his mother. The poem is structured chronologically and it starts describing when his mother is near death to the mourning for her. Heaney first describes a scene when he and his mother are peeling potatoes together, and “Little pleasant splashes / From each other’s work would bring us to our senses”(Seamus Heaney, Clearances, 3.7-8) and when his mother dies, he says “The space we stood around had been emptied/ Into us to keep, it penetrated/ Clearances that suddenly stood open/ High cries were felled and a pure change happened”(Clearances, 7.11-14) The time with his mother is filled with happiness, and when she dies, everything changes. All he experiences is the clearances. He lost his passion and hope for life. Then Heaney imagines himself going back to childhood and depicts a scene in his memory. “I thought of walking round and round a space / .../ Where the decked chestnut tree had lost its place” (Clearances, 8.1-3) Instead of talking about his mother, Heaney uses analogy and focuses on the chestnut tree. The chestnut tree is no longer there, like his mother who is no longer with him. Through his imaginative memory, he finds something new. He learns to listen to silence “A soul ramifying and forever / Silent, beyond silence listened for”(Clearances, 8) Here, Heaney describes the tree as a “ramifying soul” and he learns that even though the tree doesn’t speak, it still has a soul. It makes him realize that silence is not really silent. Having lost his mother, the world of Heaney is becoming silent. However, through the imagination, he realizes that there is something in the silence he can look for. Although the space is no longer filled with his mother’s voice, he can still listen to it and create new meanings of life. While Heaney holds on to the love for his mother, he loses the meaning of life and he doesn’t know what is the right thing to do. However, in the imaginative memory, he returns to the source and finds meaning in it. He finds a way to keep the love for his mother while still loving the world he himself lives in.

While it appears that imagination can help one persevere without surrendering either love or justice, one would argue that there is not a real conflict between justice and love in Seamus Heaney’s Clearance. Heaney describes “it penetrated clearances that suddenly stood open / High cries were felled and a pure change happened” (Clearances, 7.12-13) Some may argue that the sudden change that Heaney feels is the loss of the closeness between him and his mother rather than the loss for passion and love for his own life. However, in the beginning, the poem starts with “Teach me now to listen, / To strike it rich behind the linear back.”(Clearances,8-9) and it ends with “Silent, beyond silence listened for”(Clearances, 8.14) Here, the poem mentions “listen” both in the beginning and the end, which shows that what Heaney loses is not only the closeness with his mother, but the ability to listen. When Heaney is in grief with the loss of his mother, he is really in the fight for justice, and he doesn't really have control over whether he wants to join the “fight”. He is doing the morally right thing by mourning for his mother, and sorrow is the right emotion one should feel with the loss of a family member. In his fight for justice, Heaney loses hope and passion, which is the ability to listen. The love for his own life is exhausted by his just actions and emotions of mourning for his mother. He realizes this conflict between his just actions and love for the world. To animate his love, he learns to listen to silence through his imaginative memory.

Realizing the uncertainty and absurdity of the world, Camus and Heaney argue that the only way for one to preserve both love and justice is through imagination, in which one can return to the sources and reevaluate its meaning. In Return to Tipasa, Camus reanimates his love, which he lost after the experiencing wars, through the imaginative transcendent moments in Tipasa. In The Plague, Rieux is able to maintain both love and justice after the imaginative escape from reality. In his poem Clearances, Heaney uses imaginative memory and re-learn the meaning of life, which brought him back the love for the world. Both Camus and Heaney, by suggesting the use of imagination, are implying the absurdity of the world.
